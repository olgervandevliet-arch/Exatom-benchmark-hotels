Accessibility

Measures how usable the checkout form is for all guests: semantic HTML, label associations, ARIA attributes, keyboard navigation, and colour-contrast compliance. Hilton leads (7.6); Marriott is the outlier (2.6) on low-contrast, placeholder-only labels. Field average: 5.7. Weight in overall score: ×6.

Performance

Covers page speed and responsiveness on the guest-details / checkout page. Weight in overall score: ×5.

Style

Evaluates visual design: field alignment and sizing, label placement, font consistency, form position, and responsive layout. Marriott (7.1) and Radisson (7.0) present best – Marriott styles well even as its underlying semantics fail. Jin Jiang (Golden Tulip) is weakest (4.3). Field average: 5.6. Usability

Covers autocomplete, sensible field counts, first-field focus, keyboard navigation, CTA clarity, and avoidance of needless repetition. No brand excels here – the first field is never auto-focused, and forms carry more inputs than the task requires. Marriott trails (3.7), dragged down by disabled autocomplete. Field average: 5.5. Weight in overall score: ×6.

Web standards

The most conversion-relevant category technically: correct, semantic HTML – the right input types (e.g. type=email), real label elements, and a proper form element – driving native validation, autofill and assistive-technology support. Accor and IHG are exemplary (8.0); Marriott (0.5), Radisson (3.4) and Choice (4.2) leave easy wins on the table. Widest spread in the whole benchmark. Field average: 5.4. Weight in overall score: ×6.

Design analysis

Structural audit of guest-details / checkout forms across all 10 assessed hotel groups. Every brand is multi-step at the funnel level (four to six screens to reach guest details); at the form level nearly every brand consolidates guest information and payment onto one final page – Accor is the mild exception, separating details from payment. Field counts range from 11 (Choice Hotels) to 16 (Best Western), averaging 13 across the eight scored forms.
